Common DNS Record Explanations

Understanding the different types of DNS records is crucial when you check DNS records for troubleshooting or setup.

  • A Record: Connects a domain to an IPv4 address. Essential for web hosting.
  • AAAA Record: Similar to an A record, but connects a domain to an IPv6 address.
  • CNAME Record: A Canonical Name record aliases one domain to another (e.g., pointing www to the root domain). It cannot be placed at the root apex.
  • MX Record: Directs incoming emails to your mail server.
  • TXT Record: Used to hold text information. Commonly utilized for domain ownership verification and email security policies.
  • NS Record: Indicates which Name Servers are authoritative for the domain.
  • SOA Record: Start of Authority. Contains administrative details about the zone, such as the primary name server and administrator email.

Understanding Email Security: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC

When you perform a TXT or MX record lookup, you are often verifying email security setups. To prevent spoofing and ensure deliverability, you must configure three key TXT records:

  • SPF (Sender Policy Framework): A TXT record that lists the IP addresses and domains authorized to send email on behalf of your domain. Example: v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all
  • DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail): Adds a cryptographic signature to your emails. You publish the public key as a TXT record (e.g., selector._domainkey.yourdomain.com), which receiving servers use to verify the signature.
  • DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance): A TXT record at _dmarc.yourdomain.com that tells receiving servers what to do if an email fails SPF or DKIM checks (e.g., reject or quarantine).

MX Priority Explained

When viewing an MX record lookup, you'll notice each record has a priority number (e.g., 10, 20). Lower numbers mean higher priority. Sending mail servers will always attempt to deliver to the MX record with the lowest priority number first. If that server is unresponsive, it falls back to the next lowest number. Multiple records with the same priority will have traffic load-balanced between them.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a DNS lookup and how does it work?
A DNS lookup is the process of querying the Domain Name System to find the IP address or other records associated with a domain name. It acts like the phonebook of the internet, translating human-readable names into machine-readable IPs.
Why should I use a DNS checker?
Using a DNS checker helps you verify that your domain is pointing to the correct servers after a migration, troubleshoot website downtime, and ensure your email authentication (like SPF and DMARC) is properly configured.
How do I check DNS records for my domain?
You can check DNS records by entering your domain into our DNS lookup tool above. Select the specific record type (such as A, TXT, or MX) you want to query, and the tool will fetch the live data from multiple global resolvers.
What is an MX record lookup used for?
An MX record lookup is used to identify the mail servers responsible for receiving email on behalf of a domain. It's essential for diagnosing email delivery issues and ensuring your mail hosting is correctly linked to your domain.
How long does DNS propagation take?
DNS propagation typically takes anywhere from a few minutes to 48 hours. The time depends on the Time To Live (TTL) settings of your previous records and how frequently ISPs refresh their DNS caches.
