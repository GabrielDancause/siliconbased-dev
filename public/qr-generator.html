What is a QR Code?

A QR (Quick Response) code is a type of two-dimensional matrix barcode invented in 1994. It consists of black squares arranged in a square grid on a white background, which can be read by an imaging device such as a smartphone camera. QR codes can store significantly more data than standard barcodes, including URLs, text, contact information, and WiFi credentials.

How Error Correction Works

QR codes use Reed-Solomon error correction to ensure the code remains scannable even if it's partially damaged, obscured, or customized with a logo. There are four levels of error correction:

  • Low (L): Can recover up to 7% of data. Ideal for clean, digital displays where size must be kept small.
  • Medium (M): Can recover up to 15% of data. The standard choice for most general purposes.
  • Quartile (Q): Can recover up to 25% of data. Good for print materials in uncontrolled environments.
  • High (H): Can recover up to 30% of data. Best for adding logos to the center of the QR code or for harsh industrial environments.

Tips for Creating the Best QR Codes

  • Contrast is key: Always ensure there is a high contrast between the foreground (dots) and the background. Dark dots on a light background work best for scanners.
  • Quiet zone: Leave a sufficient margin (padding) around the QR code. The standard is 4 modules (blocks) to help scanners isolate the code from its surroundings.
  • Test before printing: Always scan your generated QR code with multiple devices before putting it on physical materials like business cards or posters.
