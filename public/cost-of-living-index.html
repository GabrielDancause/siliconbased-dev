Frequently Asked Questions

What is the cheapest city for digital nomads?

Based on our 2026 data, cities in Southeast Asia like Chiang Mai and Da Nang often rank as the most affordable, with estimated monthly living costs frequently dipping under $1,000 depending on your lifestyle choices.

How much does it cost to live in Chiang Mai?

Chiang Mai remains incredibly budget-friendly. A typical nomad can live comfortably for around $800 to $1,200 per month, which covers a modern studio apartment, daily local meals, and coworking space access.

Is Lisbon expensive for digital nomads?

Lisbon has become significantly more expensive over the last few years due to high demand. While food and transport remain relatively reasonable compared to Northern Europe, rent prices for central apartments have pushed the average nomad budget to over $2,500 per month.

What is the best city for remote workers?

The "best" city depends entirely on your priorities. For budget and community, Chiang Mai and Bali are top-tier. For European culture and timezones, Lisbon and Barcelona are favorites. For an urban lifestyle in Latin America, Medellín and Mexico City are highly rated.

How much money do you need to be a digital nomad?

To safely sustain a digital nomad lifestyle globally, a reliable income of at least $2,000 to $3,000 per month is recommended. While you can survive on less in certain regions, having a buffer ensures you can handle emergencies, flights, and enjoy your host country.

Methodology

Our 2026 Digital Nomad Cost of Living Index compiles data for 40+ popular remote work hubs. Data is aggregated from trusted crowd-sourced indices and direct price scraping where available. The "Monthly Budget" is a baseline estimate intended for a single remote worker, factoring in median rent for a 1-bedroom apartment in the city center, 60 cheap meals, 30 coffees, and a $300 buffer for groceries, coworking, and local transport. Where exact metrics could not be verified, they are marked as unavailable (null) to preserve data integrity.
